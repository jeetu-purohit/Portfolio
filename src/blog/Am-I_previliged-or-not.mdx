## Am I Privileged or Not?

Something different happened to me today—different enough to slow my thoughts in a life that rarely pauses.

Like every other morning, I was walking to the station to catch the local train for college. The route is familiar to the point of invisibility. A large bus stand sits on the way, and I pass through it almost on autopilot.

On my right, small shops were already alive. People stood in clusters, eating idli from steel plates, hurriedly swallowing breakfast before surrendering themselves to another long, exhausting workday. Some brushed past me, walking faster than usual, anxiety written in their steps—they were late for their train.

On my left, buses waited impatiently. Conductors in khaki uniforms shouted destinations in sharp, practiced Tamil, urging people to move faster, to climb in, to not waste time. Horns blared. Voices overlapped. Somewhere nearby, two people were arguing—about what, I don’t know. I was in a hurry too, so I didn’t stop to notice.

This chaos is routine. I walk past it every day without seeing it.

But today, something broke through.

Near the edge of the footpath stood a little girl—maybe eight years old. She wore an oversized T-shirt and nothing else but underwear. Her hair was fuzzy, brownish, uncombed, untouched by oil or care for a long time. In her hands was a small packet of a cheap snack. She was eating it slowly, deliberately, sharing each bite with a stray dog beside her.

I think the dog was her pet. Children on the streets often form deep, wordless bonds with animals. When the world gives you very little, companionship doesn’t demand explanations.

She belonged to the small universe of homeless families who live nearby, making the footpath their home. Yet what struck me wasn’t her poverty—it was her stillness.

All around her, life was rushing. People were running for buses and trains. Conductors were shouting. Engines were roaring. Horns were honking. Somewhere, anger was spilling into the open air.

And in the middle of all that noise, she stood completely at peace.

Just a girl.  
A dog.  
A shared packet of snacks.  
And a moment untouched by urgency.

That’s when the question hit me—quiet, heavy, unavoidable:

**Am I privileged or not?**

I have a good family. I have access to education. I have a home to return to. Food on my plate. Clothes to wear. Safety. Stability. Most of the necessities a person can reasonably ask for.

And yet, so often, my mind is occupied by what I don’t have.

I think about not owning a car.  
About not having the right environment.  
About better friends.  
About a loving girlfriend.  
About more money.  
About a life that feels slightly ahead of where I am.

I place myself on a ladder of desires and notice how many rungs are still above me. From that perspective, I don’t feel privileged at all.

But then I think of that girl.

She owns almost nothing by society’s standards. No house. No certainty. No education. No guarantees about tomorrow. Of course, she too must want things—things she doesn’t even know the names of yet.

And yet, for a brief moment, she had something I often don’t.

**Contentment.**

So where does that leave me?

I may not have enough to call myself truly privileged when I compare upward.  
But I undeniably have too much to call myself underprivileged when I compare downward.

Maybe privilege isn’t a fixed position.  
Maybe it’s a moving reference point.  
Or maybe it’s the dangerous habit of forgetting what you already have while chasing what you don’t.

As I walked away, the station pulling me back into routine, the question stayed with me—unanswered, uncomfortable, honest:

**Am I privileged or not?**
